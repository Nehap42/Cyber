Cyber safety and security

Dealing with the fake information

Fake information

Fake information is defined as false, misleading, or fabricated content that is presented as true or accurate. It may be spread either intentionally (for deception or manipulation) or unintentionally (for lack of verification). Examples include fake news, rumors, hoaxes, and altered media. It often seeks to misinform, harm reputations, or influence opinions and decisions.

The most common types of fake information used in cyberattacks:
  • Phishing Emails: Impostor emails apparently legitimate to induce users to unwittingly divulge sensitive information; for example, passwords or their financial information.
  • Fake Websites: Clone sites mirroring trusted platforms to steal passwords or spread malware.
  • Deepfakes Fake videos or recordings of audio clips generated by AIs for mimicry, black mail, spreading misinformation.
  • Fake Social Media Profiles/posting to breach trust and lead users to impart his personal information including money.
  • Rumor Messages: False warnings or offers (e.g., fake job offers, lottery winnings) sent via email, SMS, or messaging apps to lure victims.
  • Fake Software or Updates: Malicious software disguised as legitimate apps or updates to gain access to systems or data.
  • Spear Phishing: Targeted attacks using fake, personalized information to deceive specific individuals or organizations.
  • Fake News or Misinformation: Fabricated stories spread to manipulate opinions or distract from real cyber incidents.
  • Spoofed Communication: Falsified emails, caller IDs, or messages which appear to come from trusted sources to gain trust.
  • Malvertising: Fake online ads containing malicious links or software, often embedded in trusted websites.

These are methods that exploit human trust and unawareness, hence cybersecurity awareness is key. The most common form of fake information used in cyberattacks is phishing, especially phishing emails.

The attacks exploit human trust by:
  • Masquerading as legitimate organizations or individuals to make users click on Malicious links.
  • Downloading malware.
  • Gives access to sensitive information, for example, passwords, credit card information, or personal information.

Phishing is common since it is relatively simple to execute and reaches a wider audience as most of the time it uses human error rather than any technical vulnerabilities.

Dissemination of fake information in the case of cyberattacks stems from:
  • Email Services: Cyber phishing mails are forwarded through hacked or forged accounts.
  • Social Media: Spam profiles, messages, posts, and ads are used to disseminate false information or links to malicious sites.
  • Messaging Apps: Platforms such as WhatsApp, Telegram, and SMS are used to propagate hoaxes, scams, or malicious links.
  • Fake Websites: Cloned or fraudulent websites are created to steal credentials or distribute malware.
  • Online Ads (Malvertising): Malicious advertisements posted on genuine websites redirect users to harmful content.
  • Search Engines: Manipulated SEO techniques promote fake or malicious sites so that they could show in search results.
  • File Sharing Sites: Spreading malware embedded in fake software or files through cloud storage or torrent sites.
  • Spoofed Communication Channels: False emails, SMS, or calls from trusted institutions like banks and government spread malicious content.
  • Dark Web Forums: Fake content, phishing kits, and malware tools are shared among cybercriminals to spread malware content.
  • News and Media Sites: Sometimes, fake or manipulated content spreads through blogs, websites, or untrusted news channels.To counter such, individuals and organizations must always be on guard and check their sources before they believe or distribute information.
Cybersecurity Practices

The main two cyber security practice are:

Penetration:

The penetration testing, which is also called pen testing, is the cybersecurity practice of testing an organization's security measures on a regular basis. Purpose: Find vulnerabilities in the systems, networks, or applications of an organization that attackers may find and exploit them.

  • Method: Ethical hackers simulate real-life cyberattacks against defenses.
  • Outcome: Give insights into the weaknesses and recommended improvements in security.

Other related practices include vulnerability assessments and red team exercises, but penetration testing focuses on active exploitation of weaknesses.

Red teaming:

Red teaming is a practice of cybersecurity simulating real-world attacks in order to test the effectiveness of an organization's security measures. A red team acts as an adversary by using tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) that are akin to those used by actual cybercriminals or state-sponsored actors in order to identify vulnerabilities, assess defenses, and understand how well an organization can detect, respond to, and recover from attacks.

Key features

  • Simulated Attacks: It simulates a wide range of threats, including social engineering, physical breaches, network intrusions, and more.
  • Comprehensive Testing: The red team operates independently and can attack any part of an organization—systems, people, processes, and physical security.
  • No Prior Knowledge: Unlike penetration testing, red teams usually operate without sharing their plans or methodologies with the organization, making it more unpredictable.
  • Collaboration with Blue Team: After the attack simulation, the red team shares findings with the blue team (the defensive team) to help improve the organization's security posture..

Red teaming offers a more holistic and realistic view of security vulnerabilities and response effectiveness compared to traditional security assessments.

Other terms related to cyber security:
  • Vulnerability Scanning: This process of identifying vulnerabilities involves automated scanning within a system, network, or application to identify known weaknesses or vulnerabilities. It applies special tools in scanning and analysis for known security flaws that an attacker may utilize to exploit weaknesses in the systems. It therefore discovers security weaknesses and vulnerabilities even before attackers exploit them.
  • Incident Response: Incident response refers to the process of detecting, responding to, and recovering from a cybersecurity incident or breach. It is aimed at managing the situation in a manner that limits damage, reduces recovery time, and prevents future incidents. It is the efficient response to and recovery from cyberattacks or security breaches with the least damage to systems, data, and business operations.
  • Messaging Apps: Platforms such as WhatsApp, Telegram, and SMS are used to propagate hoaxes, scams, or malicious links.

Online And Social Networking Safety

Online and Social Networking Safety refers to practices and precautions taken to protect personal information, maintain privacy, and avoid threats while using the internet and social networking platforms. It involves being aware of risks such as identity theft, cyberbullying, phishing, and malware, and adopting measures to stay safe.

Key Aspects of Online and Social Networking Safety:
  • Privacy Protection: Use strong, unique passwords for online accounts.Adjust privacy settings to control who can view your posts and personal information.Avoid sharing sensitive details like home addresses, phone numbers, or financial data.
  • Avoiding Scams and Phishing: Be cautious of unsolicited messages or emails asking for personal or financial information.Avoid clicking on unknown links or downloading files from untrusted sources.
  • Securing Accounts: Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) for additional security.Regularly update passwords and use a reputable password manager.
  • Managing Online Presence: Think before posting content that might harm your reputation or violate laws. Avoid oversharing personal details that could be exploited.
  • Recognizing Cyber Threats: Be vigilant about fake profiles, frauds, or imposters on social media platforms. Report and block accounts engaging in suspicious or inappropriate activities.
  • Protecting Devices and Connections: Use antivirus software and keep your operating system and apps updated. Avoid connecting to unprotected public Wi-Fi networks without a VPN.
  • Educating Yourself and Others: Stay informed about the latest online threats and security practices. Teach children and less tech-savvy individuals about safe internet use.

By following these guidelines, you can minimize risks and enjoy a safer online and social networking experience.

Common types of cyberbullying:

Cyberbullying involves using digital platforms to harass, threaten, or embarrass someone.

  • Harassment: Repeatedly sending offensive, rude, or insulting messages.Targeting individuals with abusive comments through emails, social media, or messaging apps.
  • Impersonation (Impersonating Someone Online): Creating fake profiles or using someone else's identity to post inappropriate content or messages.Damaging the victim’s reputation by spreading false information.
  • Outing and Doxxing: Sharing someone's private, sensitive, or embarrassing information without their consent.Publishing personal details (like addresses, phone numbers, or photos) to harm or intimidate them.
  • Exclusion: Intentionally leaving someone out of online groups, chats, or activities to isolate them. Making others aware of their exclusion to amplify the emotional distress.
  • Flaming: Posting or sending aggressive, offensive, or hateful comments in online discussions or forums to provoke arguments.
  • Trolling: Deliberately posting inflammatory or off-topic content to provoke or upset someone.Often done anonymously to avoid accountability.
  • Cyberstalking: Persistently sending messages or monitoring someone's online activity to intimidate or harass them. Often includes threats of harm or invasion of privacy.
  • Fake Profiles: Creating fake accounts to humiliate or deceive others. Using these profiles to spread lies, rumors, or send threatening messages.
  • Hate Speech: Using derogatory language targeting someone’s race, gender, religion, or other personal attributes.
  • Photo or Video Shaming: Sharing unflattering or edited photos/videos of someone to embarrass or humiliate them. Using manipulated media to spread false narratives.
How to Address Cyberbullying:
  • Report and Block: Use platform tools to block and report abusive users.
  • Save Evidence: Take screenshots of abusive messages or posts for reporting purposes.
  • Privacy Settings: Strengthen privacy on social media platforms to control who can contact you.
  • Seek Support: Inform parents, teachers, or authorities, especially if it escalates.
  • Legal Action: In severe cases, consider reporting to law enforcement as cyberbullying can be a crime.

Promoting awareness and education can help prevent cyberbullying and create safer online environments.

VPN(Virtual Private Network)

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) is a technology that creates a secure and encrypted connection between your device and the internet. It routes your online activity through a private server, masking your IP address and encrypting your data to enhance security and privacy.When you connect to a VPN, your internet traffic is redirected through an encrypted tunnel to a server operated by the VPN provider.This process hides your real IP address and assigns you an IP address from the VPN server’s location.As a result, your online activities are anonymized, and your data is protected from prying eyes, including hackers, internet service providers (ISPs), and government surveillance.VPNs hide your real IP address and browsing activity, ensuring anonymity.Prevents websites and advertisers from tracking your online behaviour.

Digital footprint is the information that is left when you are using the internet. It includes both an active and passive side of information generated from online usage. There are two mainly types of digital footprints that are

Mobile safety and application safety

Application safety

Application safety, also known as application security , is a set of practices and measures designed to protect software applications from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. It encompasses the entire lifecycle of an application, from its design and development to deployment and beyond. The primary goal is to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the application and its data.

Common types of application vulnerabilities:
  • Injection flaws: These occur when user input is not properly validated or sanitized, allowing attackers to inject malicious code (e.g., SQL injection, cross-site scripting (XSS)).
  • Broken authentication and session management: Weak or improperly implemented authentication and session management mechanisms can be exploited to gain unauthorized access to user accounts or sensitive data.
  • Cross-site scripting (XSS): Attackers inject malicious scripts into web pages, which can then be executed by unsuspecting users, allowing the attacker to steal cookies, hijack sessions, or redirect users to malicious websites.
  • Broken access control: Improper access controls can allow unauthorized users to access restricted functionality or data.
  • Security misconfiguration: Incorrectly configured security settings, such as default passwords or open ports, can create vulnerabilities that attackers can exploit.
  • Sensitive data exposure: Storing or transmitting sensitive data (e.g., passwords, credit card numbers) without proper encryption or protection can lead to data breaches.
  • Insufficient logging and monitoring: Inadequate logging and monitoring can make it difficult to detect and respond to security incidents in a timely manner.
  • Using components with known vulnerabilities: Using third-party components or libraries with known vulnerabilities can expose applications to attacks.
Primary purpose of code signing for applications:

Code signing is a process that verifies the authenticity and integrity of software. It involves digitally signing the software code with a cryptographic key. The primary purposes of code signing are,

  • Authenticity: Verifies that the software comes from the claimed source and has not been tampered with.
  • Integrity: Ensures that the software has not been modified or corrupted since it was signed.
  • Trust: Provides users with confidence that the software they are installing is legitimate and comes from a trusted source.
Security practices to prevent unauthorized access to sensitive data:
  • Data encryption: Encrypting sensitive data both in transit and at rest using strong encryption algorithms (e.g., AES-256) helps protect it from unauthorized access.
  • Access control: Implement strong access controls, such as role-based access control (RBAC), to restrict access to sensitive data based on user roles and permissions.
  • Input validation and sanitization: Validate and sanitize all user input to prevent injection attacks and other vulnerabilities.
  • Secure coding practices: Follow secure coding guidelines and best practices to minimize the introduction of vulnerabilities into the application code.
  • Regular security assessments and penetration testing: Conduct regular security assessments and penetration tests to identify and address vulnerabilities.
  • Intrusion detection and prevention systems (IDPS): Deploy IDPS to monitor network traffic and detect and prevent malicious activity.
  • Regular software updates and patching: Keep the application and its underlying systems up to date with the latest security patches and updates to address known vulnerabilities.
  • Security awareness training: Educate employees about security best practices, such as strong password policies, social engineering awareness, and the importance of reporting suspicious activity.

By implementing these security measures, organizations can significantly reduce the risk of unauthorized access to sensitive data and protect their applications from cyber threats.

Mobile Safety

Mobile safety encompasses the measures and practices aimed at protecting your mobile device (smartphone or tablet) and the sensitive data it holds from various threats. That are,

  • Data Protection: Safeguarding personal information, financial details, and other confidential data stored on your device.
  • Device Security: Preventing unauthorized access to your device, such as through theft or hacking.
  • App Security: Ensuring the apps you install are legitimate and free from malware.
  • Network Security: Protecting your device and data when connected to Wi-Fi or mobile networks.
  • Physical Security: Keeping your device physically safe from loss or damage.
  • Common Mobile Security Threats
  • Malware: Malicious software like viruses, spyware, ransomware, and adware that can infect your device, steal data, or disrupt its functionality.
  • Phishing: Deceitful attempts to obtain sensitive information (like passwords and credit card details) by disguising as a trustworthy entity in emails, texts, or websites.
  • Data Breaches: Incidents where sensitive information stored on a company's servers (which may include your data) is accessed and stolen by unauthorized individuals.
  • Unsecured Wi-Fi Networks: Public Wi-Fi networks can be vulnerable to eavesdropping, allowing hackers to intercept your data.
  • Physical Theft or Loss: If your device is lost or stolen, your data can be accessed by others.
Secure Ways to Store Sensitive Data on a Mobile Device:
  • Strong Passwords: Use unique, complex passwords for each app and account. Consider a password manager to help you create and manage strong passwords securely.
  • Biometric Authentication: Utilize fingerprint, facial recognition, or iris scanning for added security.
  • Device Encryption: Encrypt your device's data to make it unreadable if it falls into the wrong hands.
  • Secure Storage Apps: Use dedicated apps designed for secure storage of sensitive information like passwords, notes, and financial details.
  • Limit App Permissions: Carefully review and grant only necessary permissions to apps you install.
Common Types of Mobile Malware:
  • Viruses: Self-replicating malware that can spread to other devices.
  • Worms: Similar to viruses but can spread independently without user interaction.
  • Spyware: Stealthily monitors user activity and collects sensitive information.
  • Ransomware: Encrypts files on your device and demands a ransom for their release.
  • Adware: Displays unwanted ads or redirects you to malicious websites.
Purpose of Mobile Security Sandbox:

A mobile security sandbox is a restricted environment within the device's operating system where apps are executed. This isolations are

  • Limits App Permissions: Prevents apps from accessing system resources or other apps without explicit authorization.
  • Contains Malware: If an app contains malware, the sandbox can limit the extent of its damage by confining its activities.
  • Enhances Device Security: Provides a layer of protection by controlling how apps interact with the device and its resources.

By understanding these aspects of mobile safety, you can take proactive steps to protect your device and data from various threats.

Password Security

Password security refers to the practices and measures used to protect passwords from unauthorized access and misuse. It's about ensuring that only the legitimate owner can use a password to gain access to accounts, systems, or data.

Best ways of setting a password:
  • Length: Aim for at least 12 characters, ideally 15 or more.
  • Complexity: Include a mix of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols.
  • Uniqueness: Create a unique password for each account. Avoid using the same password across multiple platforms.
  • Avoid Personal Information: Refrain from using personal information like birthdays, pet names, or common phrases.
  • Password Managers: Consider using a reliable password manager to generate, store, and manage strong, unique passwords for you.
Common Password Attack Techniques:
  • Brute-force attacks: Trying every possible combination of characters until the correct password is found.
  • Dictionary attacks: Using lists of common words, phrases, and names as potential passwords.
  • Phishing attacks: Tricking users into revealing their passwords through deceptive emails, messages, or websites.
  • Shoulder surfing: Visually observing someone entering their password.
  • Keyloggers: Software or hardware that records keystrokes, capturing passwords as they are typed.
Two-factor authentication:

Two-factor authentication (2FA) adds an extra layer of security by requiring two different forms of identification before granting access. This typically involves:

  • Something you know: Your password.
  • Something you have: A physical device like a security key or an authentication app on your phone.
  • Something you are: Biometric factors like fingerprint or facial recognition.

2FA significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized access, even if your password is compromised.

Purpose Of A Password Hashing Algorithm:

A password hashing algorithm transforms a plain-text password into a seemingly random string of characters (the hash). purposes are:

  • Security: Even if the hash is compromised, it's incredibly difficult to reverse-engineer the original password.
  • Storage: Hashes are stored instead of the actual passwords, making it much safer if a database is breached.
Good Password Setting Practices:
  • Regularly update passwords: Change passwords for important accounts periodically (e.g., every 3-6 months).
  • Enable 2FA:Whenever possible, enable two-factor authentication on your accounts.
  • Be cautious of phishing attempts: Be vigilant about suspicious emails, messages, and websites.
  • Use a password manager: A reliable password manager can simplify password management and enhance security.
  • Never share your passwords: Avoid sharing your passwords with anyone, even trusted friends or family.
  • Be mindful of your surroundings: Be cautious of shoulder surfing when entering passwords in public places.
